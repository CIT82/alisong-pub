About our Restaurant

A Chef's American Dream

Our History

In the heart of San Francisco's bustling Japantown, there’s a little ramen shop that’s been drawing crowds for decades — Soy Full Ramen House. The story begins in the early 1980s, when a young chef named Hiroshi Tatsuya left his hometown of Fukuoka, Japan, with a single dream: to bring the authentic flavors of Hakata ramen to the West.

Hiroshi had spent his early years training in a small but legendary ramen shop in Fukuoka, where he learned the art of crafting the perfect tonkotsu broth. His mentor, a third-generation ramen master, would often tell him, "A bowl of ramen should be simple, but it should carry the soul of the chef." It was this philosophy that Hiroshi carried with him when he boarded a plane to San Francisco in 1983.

At first, Hiroshi struggled. The ramen scene in the U.S. was nearly non-existent, and many didn't understand the depth of the dish. He started small — just a modest stall at a local food market — serving bowls of his rich, creamy tonkotsu ramen, made from pork bones simmered for over 24 hours. Word spread quickly, and it wasn’t long before lines began forming, filled with both Japanese expats yearning for a taste of home and locals curious about this new "noodle soup."

In 1987, after saving enough money, Hiroshi opened the doors to Soy Full Ramen House, a tiny, 15-seat shop with a simple wooden counter and a single goal: to serve the best ramen in San Francisco. The menu was focused, offering just three variations of ramen — Tonkotsu, Shoyu, and Miso — each made with painstaking precision.

What set Soy Full Ramen House apart was Hiroshi's dedication to the craft. Every ingredient was sourced with care, from the freshest noodles to the slow-cooked chashu pork. Hiroshi even imported his miso paste and soy sauce from Japan, refusing to compromise on quality. Locals began to affectionately refer to him as “The Ramen Sensei.”

By the 1990s, Soy Full Ramen House had become a San Francisco institution. It wasn't just a place to eat; it was an experience. Patrons would watch Hiroshi meticulously ladle broth into bowls, adding toppings with the grace of a master artist. Celebrities, food critics, and ramen enthusiasts from all over the world made pilgrimages to taste what many claimed was "the most authentic ramen outside of Japan."

Even as the ramen boom took off in the U.S., Hiroshi remained true to his roots. He never expanded or franchised, believing that ramen was a dish that required the touch of a single chef to maintain its integrity.

Today, Soy Full Ramen House still stands in its original location, a humble reminder of the passion and dedication that built it. Though Hiroshi has since passed the mantle to his son, Kenji Tatsuya, the philosophy remains the same: every bowl of ramen is made with heart, soul, and respect for tradition. Locals say that if you close your eyes while sipping the broth, you can almost hear the quiet streets of Fukuoka in every bite.

Hiroshi's Impact

Our founder's passion for ramen goes hand in hand with his commitment to giving back to the community. Beyond serving delicious bowls of ramen, he actively volunteers at local soup kitchens, helping to feed those in need. He also donates regularly to local schools, supporting educational programs and student initiatives. As a way to share his love of cooking, he offers free cooking classes, passing on his knowledge of traditional ramen-making to inspire the next generation. His dedication to making a difference is at the heart of everything we do, making our restaurant not just a place for great food, but also a force for good in the community.
